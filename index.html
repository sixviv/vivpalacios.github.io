I started my career as a software engineer — four years building production systems in Ruby on Rails, Java, and Android. I wasn't in a hurry to leave. Understanding how things actually work from the inside felt like a prerequisite, not a detour.

From there I moved into project management — not because it was the next rung, but because I was working toward product and knew I needed to understand delivery, cross-functional dynamics, and client relationships before I could own strategy. I also went for an MBA, because the business side of product — positioning, market strategy, the commercial layer — was something I wanted to understand properly, not approximate.

Six years into product management, I work at the intersection of developer experience and infrastructure: which capabilities belong on the API surface, how the response contract should be structured, which use cases warrant a streaming interface over REST, and where exposing too much complexity breaks the developer experience. That's the work at Brightcove, Wowza, and Oracle. That's the work I want to keep doing.

Based in San Francisco. Open to senior PM roles in API platforms, developer tools, B2B infrastructure, and AI products.

Product Thinking

I use LinkedIn as a thinking space, not a highlight reel. The posts here are ideas I couldn't stop turning over after a product decision, a customer conversation, or a pattern I kept seeing across teams. I share them to pressure-test the thinking with my network — not to broadcast conclusions, but to open the discussion. If something resonates or you disagree, I want to hear it.

May 27, 2026
shower thought... I've seen a lot of products lately where the answer to every user problem is: "we have an AI agent for that". More context grounding. Better RAG. More tools. More function calls. And sometimes that's right. But we're often reaching for the most sophisticated tool in the toolbox before asking the simpler question: should AI own this outcome at all? The real PM skill in an AI-native world isn't knowing how to spec an agent. It's knowing when not to. AI maximalism isn't a product strategy. Selectivity is. The new product judgment isn't "how do I add AI to this?" — it's "what should AI actually own here, and what falls apart if it does?" —— What's your mental model for deciding what goes to AI vs. stays deterministic?

One Platform: Bridging
On-Premise and Cloud

How I identified a retention risk among on-premise customers and led the product initiative that enabled them to access cloud capabilities — increasing cross-product adoption and reducing churn.

Customer Retention Enterprise SaaS Platform Strategy Live Streaming Cross-Product Adoption

01 · Context

Two Products, One Fragmented Customer Base

A live streaming platform operated two parallel product lines: a legacy on-premise solution used by established enterprise customers, and a newer cloud-based SaaS OVP built with modern capabilities. The two products existed independently — customers were on one or the other, never both. As the cloud product matured, it widened the capability gap: cloud customers got faster delivery of new features while on-premise customers watched their solution fall behind.

02 · Problem Statement

The Challenge

On-premise customers represented significant ARR but faced a structural disadvantage: every new cloud-native capability was unavailable to them. Their alternatives were either migrating entirely to cloud (high cost, high disruption for established enterprise environments) or switching to a competitor who could offer a hybrid path.

Why it matters: Losing an on-premise enterprise customer isn't just lost revenue — it's a signal that the platform is treating a segment as legacy rather than investing in its longevity. In a competitive market, that signal travels.

"On-premise customers had made infrastructure investments in our platform. The product's job was to honor that investment while giving them a path forward — not forcing them to choose between our cloud product and a competitor."

03 · Customer Research

Who We Were Talking To

🏢

Enterprise On-Prem Customer

Large organization with existing infrastructure investment and long procurement cycles. Wants access to cloud features without a full migration.

"We need cloud encryption support but we can't migrate our whole infrastructure this quarter."

🔄

Hybrid Evaluator

Actively evaluating cloud-native competitors. Would stay if they could access key cloud features without disrupting their on-prem setup.

"Your competitor offers this natively. What's your path?"

📈

Expansion-Ready Customer

Has grown beyond on-prem capacity and wants to extend to cloud for burst workloads, but doesn't want two separate vendor relationships.

"We'd expand if we could manage both environments from one place."

04 · Approach

Discovery & Alignment

1

Direct Customer Engagement

Conducted working sessions with on-premise enterprise accounts to map their integration challenges. The goal was to understand which cloud features mattered most and what technical barriers prevented adoption — not to validate a solution we'd already decided on.

2

Win/Loss Analysis

Synthesized win/loss data with the marketing team to identify deals where the on-prem/cloud divide was a deciding factor. This surfaced how often competitors were winning by offering hybrid capability out of the box.

3

Cross-Functional Alignment

Brought engineering, sales, marketing, and support into the framing early. Each team had a different view of the on-prem customer's experience — sales saw churn risk, support saw integration pain, engineering saw technical debt. The initiative needed all four perspectives to define the right scope.

4

Technical Feasibility Assessment

Worked with engineering to determine which cloud features could be surfaced to on-prem customers via API bridge, which required deeper integration work, and which were architecturally incompatible in the near term. This scoped the MVP without overpromising.

05 · Root Causes

Why Customers Were at Risk of Churning

Feature Parity Divergence

Cloud product shipped new capabilities faster than they could be backported to on-prem. The gap widened over time with no clear bridge.

No Hybrid Path

Customers who wanted to extend to cloud had to treat it as a separate product — separate contracts, separate integrations, separate support relationships.

Critical Focus

Platform Felt Like a Dead End

On-premise customers perceived no investment in their product line. Without a visible roadmap toward cloud feature access, the default conclusion was that the platform was deprioritizing them in favor of cloud-native customers.

Integration Complexity as a Blocker

Even customers who wanted to adopt cloud capabilities faced high integration complexity — two separate APIs, separate authentication, and no unified management interface.

06 · Solution

A Cloud Feature Access Layer for On-Premise Customers

The initiative enabled on-premise customers to selectively leverage cloud capabilities — specifically content encryption and cloud delivery features — without requiring a full migration. The approach was additive: customers kept their existing on-prem infrastructure while gaining a controlled path to cloud features through a unified integration layer.

01

Cloud Encryption for On-Prem Content

On-premise customers could route content through cloud-based encryption workflows — enabling multi-DRM support without migrating their storage or delivery infrastructure.

02

Unified Integration Layer

A single API surface bridging on-prem and cloud products. Customers could manage cloud feature configuration from their existing integration without a separate cloud account setup.

03

Incremental Adoption Model

Customers could adopt individual cloud features independently — not forced into a full cloud migration. Each feature adoption became a natural step toward a fuller hybrid or cloud deployment.

07 · Metrics

How We Measured Success

Success Metrics
On-premise customer churn rate during the initiative window
Cloud feature adoption rate among existing on-prem customers
Cross-product ARR expansion from on-prem accounts adopting cloud features
Time-to-first-cloud-feature for on-prem customers (integration complexity proxy)
Win rate improvement in competitive deals where hybrid capability was a factor
Negative Impact to Watch
Support burden — hybrid integrations increase complexity for the support team handling mixed on-prem/cloud configurations
Migration slowdown — if on-prem customers can access key cloud features, they may defer full cloud migration, reducing cloud ARR growth
Integration fragility — bridge layer becomes a long-term maintenance burden if not properly scoped and owned

08 · Rollout Strategy

Phased Delivery

Phase 1 — Targeted

High-Risk Account Cohort

  • Identify on-prem accounts showing churn signals (low engagement, competitor evaluation)
  • Prioritized access to cloud encryption feature for this cohort
  • Dedicated onboarding support to reduce integration friction
  • Qualitative feedback loop with each account
Phase 2 — Expand

Broader On-Prem Base

  • Expand cloud feature access to all on-prem customers
  • Self-serve integration documentation published
  • Sales enablement update: hybrid capability now a supported sales motion
  • Support team trained on hybrid configuration scenarios
Phase 3 — Mature

Platform Integration

  • Hybrid feature access reflected in unified product roadmap
  • Cloud feature adoption tracked in customer health scoring
  • Renewal conversations enriched with usage data from both on-prem and cloud
  • Roadmap communication to on-prem customers restored confidence in platform investment

09 · Outcome & Learnings

What This Built

The initiative increased cross-product adoption among on-premise customers and reduced churn in the targeted cohort. But the less quantifiable outcome may have been more important: it restored trust. On-premise customers had begun to feel de-prioritized by a platform that was visibly investing in cloud. Shipping a concrete bridge — and communicating it through the roadmap — sent the signal that the platform was investing in their success, not waiting for them to leave.

The key learning: retention is often a product communication problem before it becomes a product gap problem. Customers don't churn the day a feature is missing — they churn when they stop believing the missing feature is coming. Closing that belief gap is as important as closing the feature gap.
